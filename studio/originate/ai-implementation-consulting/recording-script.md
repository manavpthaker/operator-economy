## THESIS

[exhales]

Okay, so. Here's the thesis.

Every business out there now knows it's supposed to be using AI. Every single one. And almost none of them know how.

That gap? Between knowing and doing?

[short pause]

That gap is a service business. It's got a name. It's called implementation.

And look — let's be clear about what this is not. You are not building models. You're not out there raising venture capital. None of that. What you're doing is... you're taking tools that already exist, tools that are sitting right there, and you're wiring them into how a real business actually runs. Its intake. Its follow-ups. Its reporting.

[inhales]

I spent six years building product for hospitality businesses. Six years. And the pattern was everywhere — technology that got bought, half-installed, and quietly... abandoned.

Here's the thing though. The gap was never the software.

It was the person. The person who'd stand there until it actually ran.

And the reason — the reason this is buildable by one person, right now, by one person — is that the installation tools went no-code. What used to take a systems integrator and six months? That's a workflow you can stand up in an afternoon.

[short pause]

Which raises the obvious question.

If it's that easy... why is Accenture charging billions for it?

## EVIDENCE

Start at the top of the market, because it proves demand. Accenture's generative AI bookings roughly doubled — to five point nine billion dollars — in fiscal twenty twenty-five, and revenue from that work roughly tripled, to two point seven billion.

Here's the detail that matters more: their overall new bookings were flat. The AI implementation work is the only thing growing. By late twenty twenty-five they stopped reporting it separately, because it touched eighty percent of large deals.

Now the other end of the market. And look — there's a reported model making the rounds. A solo automation agency, forty thousand dollars a month, five clients, on retainers.

I want to be careful here. That number comes from a creator-economy blog, not audited books. Treat it as the ceiling story. Not the median.

The floor's better documented, because it's boring — freelancers doing AI automation at five to six thousand a month, on about twenty dollars of tooling.

And the pricing converges. Across every source I found. Two to five thousand for a first project. Five hundred to five thousand a month for retainers.

One more structural signal, from the enterprise side. About half of these AI projects pull through additional data and systems work. Services attach to services, right? Your two thousand dollar install becomes a relationship. Not a transaction.

Every install my team ever did surfaced three problems we hadn't been hired to fix. The reservation fix exposed the inventory mess. The inventory fix exposed the reporting mess.

That's not scope creep.

[short pause]

That's the business model.

So the same business exists at three scales. Accenture's billions. Boutique agencies' reported six figures. A freelancer's few thousand a month.

The service is identical. The only variable... is who's buying.

## STACK

Okay, so let's talk about the stack. Because people think this part is expensive. It is not.

Whole thing costs less than a hundred dollars a month.

The brain is Claude or ChatGPT — twenty to forty bucks. That's your analysis, your drafts, and... increasingly, the build itself.

The runtime is n8n, Make, or Zapier. That's the thing actually executing the workflows for the client. n8n self-hosted is close to free — which matters, right? When you're quoting margins, that matters.

Then the client-facing layer — Airtable or Notion. That's the dashboard they log into. The thing that makes them feel ownership.

And here's the thing — this is the part clients think they're paying for. So make it clean.

Oh, and Loom, for async delivery. Recorded walkthroughs instead of meetings.

Every tool here has a free tier. So your cost of goods on a two thousand dollar project? Rounds to zero.

Which means the entire game... is finding the first client.

And that's where most people get it backwards.

## PLAYBOOK

Okay, so — the playbook. Week one.

Pick one vertical you've actually worked in. Not "small businesses" — that's not a vertical, that's a cop-out. One industry whose problems you can name from memory.

And every source on this business, all of them, converge on the same point: vertical focus. Mine would be hotels and restaurants — built for them for years, I can name their intake problems in my sleep.

Yours is whatever industry you can argue with.

Weeks two to four: build two, maybe three real case studies. Do them cheap. Or free, even, for businesses you already know.

Here's the thing though — you're not donating work. You're buying proof. And proof is the only marketing this business needs. Full stop.

Then you package it. A fixed-scope offer: one named problem, one price — somewhere between two and five thousand — one deadline.

Because businesses don't buy "automation." Nobody wants automation. They buy "your missed calls get answered and booked." That's the sentence.

The first client comes from your existing network. And that's not a hack, right? That's the model. Operators buy from operators. Your credibility — the stuff you built in the industry you left — that's the actual asset here.

And after the install, you offer the retainer. Maintenance, plus one new workflow a month. Five hundred to a few thousand, depending on scope.

Remember the attach effect. The first project? It's designed to surface the second.
