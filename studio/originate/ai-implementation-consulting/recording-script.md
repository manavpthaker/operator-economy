## EVIDENCE

Okay, let's start at the top of the market, because it proves demand.

Accenture's generative AI bookings roughly doubled to five point nine billion dollars in fiscal twenty twenty-five. Revenue from that work roughly tripled to two point seven billion.

Here's the detail that matters more though. Their overall new bookings were flat. The AI implementation work is the only thing growing. And by late twenty twenty-five they stopped reporting it separately because it touched eighty percent of large deals.

Now the other end of the market.

There's a reported model making the rounds. A solo automation agency at forty thousand dollars a month from five clients on retainers. I want to be careful here, that number comes from a creator-economy blog, not audited books. Treat it as the ceiling story, not the median.

The floor is better documented because it's boring. Freelancers doing AI automation at five to six thousand a month on about twenty dollars of tooling. And pricing converges across every source I found. Two to five thousand for a first project, five hundred to five thousand a month for retainers.

One more structural signal from the enterprise side. About half of these AI projects pull through additional data and systems work. Services attach to services, right? Your two thousand dollar install becomes a relationship, not a transaction.

Every install my team ever did surfaced three problems we hadn't been hired to fix. The reservation fix exposed the inventory mess. The inventory fix exposed the reporting mess.

That's not scope creep. That's the business model.

So the same business exists at three scales. Accenture's billions, boutique agencies' reported six figures, and a freelancer's few thousand a month. The service is identical. The only variable is who's buying.

## STACK

The whole stack costs less than a hundred dollars a month.

The brain is Claude or ChatGPT, twenty to forty bucks. That's your analysis, your drafts, and increasingly the build itself.

The runtime is n8n, Make, or Zapier. That's the thing that actually executes workflows for the client. n8n self-hosted is close to free, and that matters when you're quoting margins.

The client-facing layer is Airtable or Notion. The dashboard they log into, the thing they feel ownership of. This is the part clients think they're paying for, right? So make it clean.

And Loom for async delivery. Recorded walkthroughs instead of meetings.

Every tool here has a free tier. So your cost of goods on a two thousand dollar project rounds to zero.

Which means the entire game is finding the first client. And that's where most people get it backwards.

## PLAYBOOK

Okay so, the playbook. Week one: pick one vertical you've actually worked in. Not "small businesses." One industry whose problems you can name from memory.

And look, every source on this business says the same thing here. Vertical focus. Mine would be hotels and restaurants, because I built for them for years and I can name their intake problems in my sleep. Yours is whatever industry you can argue with.

Weeks two to four: build two or three real case studies. Do them cheap or free for businesses you already know.

And here's the thing, you're not donating work. You're buying proof. And proof is the only marketing this business needs.

Then package. A fixed scope offer. One named problem, one price between two and five thousand, one deadline.

Businesses don't buy "automation." They buy "your missed calls get answered and booked."

The first client comes from your existing network, right? And that's not a hack. That's the model. Operators buy from operators. Your credibility from the industry you left, that's the actual asset here.

And after the install, offer the retainer. Maintenance plus one new workflow a month. Five hundred to a few thousand, depending on scope.

Remember the attach effect. The first project is designed to surface the second.
