# Deep research prompt: YouTube craft — what worked, what works now

Goal of the output: raw material for a scoreable content rubric (hooks, titles, thumbnails, structure, retention) that we can run every Operator Economy script/package against before publish — analogous to the rubric used for Manav's personal LinkedIn content.

---

I run a thesis-led business/AI explainer YouTube channel (10-minute documentary-style videos with voiceover, motion graphics, and charts; MagnatesMedia / How Money Works / Starter Story adjacent; audience: senior professionals 35–55 in career transition, allergic to hustle-hype). I need a research-backed craft playbook — what made videos in this genre work historically (2019–2024), and what works NOW (2025–2026, post-AI-flood, post-inauthentic-content enforcement). Prioritize creator-disclosed analytics, platform-official guidance (YouTube Creator Insider, published retention studies), and teardown analyses of specific successful videos over generic listicle advice. For every claim, distinguish evidence-backed findings from folklore.

1. **Hooks (first 30 seconds).** (a) Deconstruct the opening 30 seconds of the 10 highest-performing videos in business-explainer/documentary niches from the last 18 months: transcribe the structures (cold open? question? number-first? stakes-first? in-medias-res?), time-stamp when the premise is fully stated, and note what's on screen vs what's said. (b) What does retention data say about the first 30s — what drop-off is normal, what's elite, and which specific hook patterns correlate with flatter opening curves? (c) How have hooks changed 2024→2026 — is the "you won't believe" era measurably dead in professional niches, and what replaced it? (d) Extract 5–8 named, repeatable hook archetypes for this genre with a real example of each.

2. **Titles.** (a) Analyze title patterns of top business/finance/AI explainers: length, number usage, specificity vs curiosity gap, first-word choices, capitalization norms, "How X" vs "Why X" vs declarative claims — with view-performance evidence where creators have disclosed A/B results. (b) What does YouTube's own title A/B testing feature data reveal (creator-disclosed test results)? (c) Which title patterns now read as AI-slop signals to viewers and suppress clicks in professional niches? (d) Produce a scoring checklist: 6–10 binary/graded title criteria with rationale.

3. **Thumbnails.** (a) Current best evidence on thumbnail CTR drivers in faceless/graphics-led channels specifically (no creator face available): text word-count limits, number prominence, color contrast systems, single-focal-point rules, mobile legibility at 320px. (b) Teardowns of MagnatesMedia, How Money Works, Economics Explained, Starter Story thumbnail systems — what's consistent across their libraries? (c) CTR benchmarks: what's median vs good vs elite CTR in these niches at small-channel scale (<10K subs), and how does CTR interact with impressions ramp on new channels? (d) Scoring checklist: 5–8 thumbnail criteria.

4. **Structure & retention through the body.** (a) What retention-curve shapes do successful 8–12 minute explainers show, and where are the predictable cliff points (intro end, mid-roll, section transitions)? (b) Evidence on re-hooking techniques: open loops, chapter previews, pattern interrupts, B-roll/graphic change frequency, pacing of visual changes per minute — which have measured effects? (c) Does explicit chaptering help or hurt watch time in this genre? (d) Optimal ordering: does payoff-first (conclusion up front) beat build-up in professional explainers, per any disclosed data? (e) End-screen/CTA placement: when do CTAs measurably bleed retention, and what's the evidence on lead-magnet CTAs mid-video vs end?

5. **What changed in 2025–2026 specifically.** (a) Post-AI-flood viewer behavior: is there measurable "AI fatigue" — skepticism signals toward synthetic voice, stock-footage montage, generic pacing — in professional niches? (b) What are surviving/growing channels doing differently in craft terms since the July 2025 inauthentic-content policy (human voice, on-screen sourcing, imperfection markers, "no AI" positioning)? (c) How has the recommendation system's weighting shifted (creator-observed): CTR vs average view duration vs velocity vs returning-viewer share? (d) Small-channel cold start 2026: what craft factors most determine whether the first 20 videos get test impressions?

6. **Shorts craft (discovery layer only).** (a) Hook patterns for business/finance Shorts with disclosed swipe-away data — what survives the first 2 seconds? (b) Evidence on Shorts→long-form conversion mechanics: pinned comments, "full video" end-cards, title mirroring. (c) What Shorts craft actively damages a professional brand (and should be banned in our rubric)?

7. **Output — build the rubric raw material:** (a) A master table: every evidence-backed craft finding, its source/confidence level, and the measurable criterion it implies. (b) A draft 100-point content rubric split across Hook (25), Title (15), Thumbnail (15), Structure/Retention (25), Voice/Trust (10), CTA/Conversion (10) — each criterion binary or 3-level, scoreable from the script + package alone, before publish. (c) A "kill list" of patterns that tested well in 2020–2023 but now underperform or signal slop. (d) The 5 metrics to check at 24h/7d/28d post-publish that validate or falsify each rubric dimension, so the rubric self-corrects against our own analytics.

Context for calibration: channel is "The Operator Economy" — evidence-first business blueprints, no income promises, human-written analysis with AI-assisted production, blueprint lead magnet per video, LinkedIn/newsletter seeding for early distribution. The rubric will be enforced pre-publish the way we enforce script rigor (sources, no hype, POV requirements), so criteria must be checkable from the artifact, not vibes.
